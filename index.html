02 — The Problem

A product admins dreaded logging into

Our enterprise customers manage hundreds — sometimes thousands — of seats across departments, contractors, and partner orgs. When access goes wrong, it's a compliance emergency. The previous RBAC interface was built by engineers, for engineers. Every capability exposed. Every edge case a toggle. The result looked more like a server config file than a product.

"I know the settings are in there somewhere. I just… don't touch it unless something breaks."

— Org Admin, discovery interview

PAIN 01

Invisible risk

No signals on dangerous permission overlaps. Everything looked equally important.

PAIN 02

Audit panic

Compliance queries required manual CSV digs. Average time to answer: 47 minutes.

PAIN 03

Multi-tenant blindness

No unified view across orgs. Admins switched contexts one-by-one, losing state each time.

PAIN 04

Fear of clicking

78% of test participants paused before confirming any permission change. Cognitive load was destroying confidence.

03 — Product Screens

Three surfaces, one coherent system

Each tab has a distinct job — editing, monitoring, and understanding. Mixing them created visual competition. Separating them let each surface breathe.

Design Principles
01
State over metaphor
No lock icons, no green/red anxiety. Binary states communicate clearly without emotional noise.
02
Friction as feature
Explicit publish. Exit warnings. On high-stakes surfaces, confirmation steps protect users.
03
Context never collapses
Org selector is always visible. Role accents are consistent. Users always know where they are.
04
Density without noise
8 resources × 6 roles × 5 actions = 240 cells. Designed for scan, not reading. Every pixel earns its place.

What changed between lo-fi and shipped

The matrix survived structurally intact. The audit log's right-panel variant was cut after three usability sessions — inline expansion won. The role overview had no seat distribution bar until Week 6, when admins kept asking "which roles are most common?" during testing.

07 — Research & Tools

14 sessions. 9 enterprise accounts. One reframe.

The insight that changed everything: admins didn't need to see capabilities — they needed to see consequences. The question was never "what can I configure?" It was "if I do this, what breaks?"

Discovery Methods
1

14 user interviews

Only 3 of 14 could correctly explain what a Billing role could access without looking it up. The system was illegible.

2

Session shadowing

No admin used the audit log proactively. It existed as evidence, not intelligence. That reframe became a design pillar.

3

Competitive analysis

Okta, Rippling, Azure AD — all powerful but require training. Opportunity: clarity at scale, not just capability.

08 — Design Tradeoffs

Every real decision, unfiltered

Each of these decisions had a real cost. Here's what was chosen and why.

DecisionRejectedChosenWhy
Permission UIDrag-and-drop natural language builderMatrix GridNL tested 40% slower for bulk edits. Enterprise admins think in spreadsheets.
Save modelAuto-save with undo historyExplicit PublishAuto-save caused 3 critical errors in testing. The friction is the feature.
Audit detailRight-side detail panelInline Row ExpandPanel compressed table on 13" screens. Inline keeps context, no navigation.
Audit streamReal-time WebSocket feedPaginated + FiltersLive updates caused scroll loss and anxiety in testing. Stability > recency.
Org contextSeparate dashboards per tenantUnified Dropdown18% of sessions spent switching. Single-org users see unused control — acceptable waste.
Role overviewSidebar inside matrix tabSeparate TabMatrix = task surface. Overview = monitoring surface. Mixing creates visual competition.
Granularity5 preset permission tiersFull CRUD + ExportEnterprise RFPs required custom granularity. Complexity kept, hidden by smart defaults.
The call I'm still not sure about

We cut the AI permission risk score from v1

We prototyped a feature that flagged dangerous combos (Developer + Billing Export = data leak vector). Engineering loved it. Security loved it. In testing, admins felt surveilled. We cut it. It belongs on the roadmap — but trust has to be earned before guardrails make sense.

10 — Reflection

What I'd do differently

The AI risk score we cut? I should have shipped a read-only version — no blocking, just a subtle signal. I overcorrected on the trust concern. Post-launch feedback showed admins would have welcomed it, especially opt-in.

I also underinvested in onboarding for admins inheriting an existing permission structure. We designed for people setting up from scratch. Most enterprise admins inherit a mess and need to understand it before they can change it. That job-to-be-done was left entirely unserved.

Good design ships knowingly incomplete. You just have to know which parts you left out.
